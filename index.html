Formal Fallacies

Errors in the logical structure of an argument, regardless of content.

Formal

Affirming the Consequent

If A then B. B is true. Therefore A is true. (Invalid: B could have other causes.)
Example: "If it rains, the street is wet. The street is wet. Therefore, it rained." (A sprinkler could cause wet streets.)
Counter: Ask "What else could cause B?" Always consider alternative explanations.
Formal

Denying the Antecedent

If A then B. A is false. Therefore B is false. (Invalid: B may still be true for other reasons.)
Example: "If you study hard, you'll pass. You didn't study hard. Therefore, you won't pass." (Natural talent could suffice.)
Counter: The conditional only states sufficiency, not necessity. Identify other sufficient conditions.
Formal

Undistributed Middle

All A are C. All B are C. Therefore all A are B. (Invalid: sharing a property doesn't create identity.)
Example: "All dogs are animals. All cats are animals. Therefore all dogs are cats." The shared category doesn't connect them.
Counter: Draw a Venn diagram. Shared supercategories don't imply overlap.
Formal

False Dilemma (False Dichotomy)

Presenting only two options when more exist. Artificially constraining the choice set.
Example: "You're either with us or against us." (Many positions exist between total support and opposition.)
Counter: Ask "Are these really the only two options?" Generate a third alternative. Use KeepRule Scenarios to explore multi-option decisions.
Formal

Masked Man Fallacy

Substituting identical things in belief contexts. Just because X=Y factually doesn't mean beliefs about X transfer to Y.
Example: "I know my neighbor. The masked robber is my neighbor. Therefore I know the masked robber." (You don't know them AS the robber.)
Counter: Distinguish between factual identity and epistemic (knowledge) contexts.
Formal

Existential Fallacy

Drawing a conclusion about existing things from premises about categories that may be empty.
Example: "All unicorns are magical. Therefore, some things are magical." (Unicorns don't exist, so the conclusion doesn't follow.)
Counter: Verify that the category being discussed actually has members. Check base reality before reasoning.

Fallacies of Relevance

Arguments where the premises are logically irrelevant to the conclusion.

Relevance

Ad Hominem

Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself.
Example: "You can't trust his economic analysis because he went bankrupt once." (Past failure doesn't invalidate current reasoning.)
Counter: Refocus on the argument: "Let's evaluate the evidence, not the person."
Relevance

Straw Man

Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack.
Example: A: "We should invest more in renewable energy." B: "So you want to ban all fossil fuels immediately and destroy the economy?"
Counter: Restate the original argument accurately: "That's not what I said. My actual position is..."
Relevance

Appeal to Authority

Claiming something is true because an authority figure said so, especially when the authority is outside their expertise.
Example: "This famous physicist endorses this diet plan, so it must work." (Physics expertise doesn't transfer to nutrition.)
Counter: Is the authority actually an expert in this field? What does the evidence say? Check expert credentials.
Relevance

Appeal to Emotion

Using emotional manipulation instead of logical evidence to support a conclusion.
Example: "Think of the children!" used to shut down policy debate without addressing the actual merits of the policy.
Counter: Acknowledge the emotion, then redirect: "I share that concern. Now let's look at the evidence about what actually helps."
Relevance

Tu Quoque (Whataboutism)

Deflecting criticism by pointing out the critic's own inconsistency, without addressing the argument.
Example: "You say I should exercise more? Well, you don't exercise either!" (Your habits don't make the advice wrong.)
Counter: "Whether I practice it or not doesn't change whether the advice is sound. Let's discuss the merits."
Relevance

Red Herring

Introducing an irrelevant topic to divert attention from the original issue.
Example: When asked about budget overruns: "But look at all the jobs we've created!" (Doesn't address the budget question.)
Counter: "That's interesting, but the question was about [original topic]. Can we address that first?"
Relevance

Appeal to Popularity (Bandwagon)

Arguing something is true or good because many people believe it or do it.
Example: "Millions of people use this product, so it must be the best." (Popularity doesn't equal quality.)
Counter: Evaluate based on evidence, not popularity. Apply decision frameworks from KeepRule Principles.
Relevance

Genetic Fallacy

Judging something based solely on its origin rather than its current merit.
Example: "That idea came from our competitor, so we shouldn't consider it." (Evaluate the idea on its merits.)
Counter: Separate the source from the substance. Good ideas can come from unexpected places.

Fallacies of Presumption

Arguments that assume something that hasn't been proven.

Presumption

Begging the Question (Circular Reasoning)

Using the conclusion as a premise. The argument assumes what it's trying to prove.
Example: "God exists because the Bible says so. The Bible is true because it's the word of God." The argument is circular.
Counter: Identify the circular dependency: "Your premise assumes your conclusion. Can you provide independent evidence?"
Presumption

Slippery Slope

Claiming one event will inevitably lead to extreme consequences without demonstrating the causal chain.
Example: "If we allow remote work Fridays, soon no one will come to the office at all, and the company culture will collapse."
Counter: Challenge each link: "What evidence shows step A leads to step B?" Most slopes aren't as slippery as claimed.
Presumption

Hasty Generalization

Drawing broad conclusions from insufficient or unrepresentative examples.
Example: "I met two rude people from that city. Everyone there must be rude." (Two people is not a representative sample.)
Counter: Ask "Is this sample large and representative enough?" Consider base rates. Use data-driven thinking.
Presumption

False Cause (Post Hoc)

Assuming that because B followed A, A caused B. Confusing correlation with causation.
Example: "I wore my lucky socks and we won the game. My socks caused the win." (Correlation, not causation.)
Counter: Look for alternative explanations, confounding variables, and controlled studies.
Presumption

Composition Fallacy

Assuming what's true of the parts is true of the whole.
Example: "Every player on this team is excellent, so the team must be excellent." (Great individuals don't always make a great team.)
Counter: Emergent properties exist. Evaluate the whole separately from its parts.
Presumption

Division Fallacy

Assuming what's true of the whole is true of each part.
Example: "This company is profitable, so every department must be profitable." (Some departments may lose money.)
Counter: Properties of wholes don't necessarily distribute to parts. Examine individual components.
Presumption

Loaded Question

Asking a question that contains an unproven assumption, making any direct answer an admission.
Example: "Have you stopped cheating on your taxes?" Any yes/no answer admits past cheating.
Counter: Reject the premise: "I reject the assumption in your question. The facts are..."
Presumption

No True Scotsman

Redefining the group to exclude counterexamples rather than accepting the counterexample.
Example: "No real entrepreneur would fail." When shown failures: "Well, they weren't REAL entrepreneurs."
Counter: "You're moving the goalposts. By the original definition, this IS a counterexample."
🌀

Fallacies of Ambiguity & Miscellaneous

Arguments that exploit vagueness, ambiguity, or other structural issues.

Ambiguity

Equivocation

Using a word with multiple meanings in different parts of the argument as if it means the same thing.
Example: "The sign said 'fine for parking here,' so I parked. It was fine!" ("Fine" means penalty in one context, acceptable in another.)
Counter: Define key terms precisely at the start of any argument. Clarify ambiguous language.
Ambiguity

Appeal to Nature

Arguing that something is good because it's natural, or bad because it's unnatural.
Example: "This medicine is natural, so it's safe." (Many natural substances are deadly: arsenic, hemlock, etc.)
Counter: "Natural" and "good" are separate evaluations. Evaluate on evidence, not origin.
Ambiguity

Sunk Cost Fallacy

Continuing an endeavor because of previously invested resources rather than future expected value.
Example: "We've already spent $5M on this project. We can't stop now." (The $5M is gone regardless. Only future costs and benefits matter.)
Counter: Ask "If I were starting fresh today, would I invest in this?" Use KeepRule for objective decision framing.
Ambiguity

Gambler's Fallacy

Believing that past random events affect future probabilities in independent events.
Example: "The coin has landed heads 5 times in a row, so tails is 'due.'" (Each flip is independent: still 50/50.)
Counter: Independent events have no memory. Past outcomes don't change future probabilities.
Ambiguity

Appeal to Ignorance

Arguing that a claim is true because it hasn't been proven false (or vice versa).
Example: "No one has proven aliens DON'T exist, so they must exist." (Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, but neither is it proof.)
Counter: The burden of proof lies with the person making the positive claim.
Ambiguity

Middle Ground Fallacy

Assuming the truth lies between two extreme positions simply because it's a compromise.
Example: Person A says the earth is round. Person B says it's flat. "The truth must be somewhere in between." (No. One is simply correct.)
Counter: Evaluate each claim on its merits. Truth isn't determined by splitting the difference.
Ambiguity

Survivorship Bias

Drawing conclusions from visible successes while ignoring the much larger pool of invisible failures.
Example: "College dropouts like Gates and Zuckerberg became billionaires, so dropping out leads to success." (Ignores millions of unsuccessful dropouts.)
Counter: Always ask "Where are the failures?" Consider the full dataset. See expert perspectives on KeepRule.
Ambiguity

Nirvana Fallacy

Rejecting a solution because it isn't perfect, comparing real options to unrealistic ideals.
Example: "This safety measure won't prevent ALL accidents, so it's not worth implementing." (Reducing accidents by 80% is still valuable.)
Counter: Compare options to each other and the status quo, not to perfection. Visit KeepRule FAQ for practical decision guidance.

How to Use This Handbook

Knowing fallacies is only useful if you apply that knowledge in real conversations and decisions.

Step 1: Listen Actively

Before identifying fallacies in others' arguments, make sure you truly understand what they are saying. Steelmanning - presenting the strongest version of their argument - is more productive than strawmanning.
Practice: In your next meeting, summarize others points before responding. This builds trust and ensures you address the real argument, not a distorted version.

Step 2: Check Your Own Reasoning

The biggest value of knowing fallacies is catching them in your own thinking. We are all susceptible to motivated reasoning and cognitive shortcuts.
Practice: Before making a major decision, run through this checklist: Am I using circular reasoning? Am I attacking the person not the idea? Am I assuming a false dichotomy? Visit KeepRule Scenarios for practice.

Step 3: Respond Constructively

When you spot a fallacy, name it gently and redirect to the evidence. Saying "that is a logical fallacy" in a confrontational way rarely persuades anyone. Instead, ask questions that guide them to see the gap in reasoning.
Practice: Replace "That is a straw man" with "I want to make sure my position is understood correctly. What I am actually saying is..." This achieves the same goal without triggering defensiveness.

Step 4: Build a Reasoning Habit

Critical thinking is a skill that improves with practice. Keep this handbook bookmarked and review one fallacy per day. Over time, pattern recognition becomes automatic.
Practice: Start a reasoning journal. When you encounter a persuasive argument, analyze it for fallacies before accepting it. Combine with structured decision principles for maximum clarity.

Fallacy Detection in Daily Life

In Advertising

Advertisements frequently use appeals to emotion, authority, and popularity. "9 out of 10 dentists recommend" combines appeal to authority with bandwagon. "Feel the luxury" is pure emotional appeal without evidence of quality.
Defense: Ask what objective evidence supports the claim, independent of how it makes you feel or who endorses it.

In Business Meetings

Sunk cost fallacy dominates corporate decision-making. Teams continue failing projects because of past investment. False dichotomies limit creative solutions. Ad hominem attacks disguised as "concerns about experience" derail good proposals.
Defense: Frame every decision as starting fresh: "Given where we are today, what is the best path forward?" Use tools from KeepRule Blog.

In Personal Finance

Anchoring bias makes us judge prices relative to arbitrary reference points rather than objective value. Survivorship bias makes us overweight success stories. The gambler fallacy makes us think a losing streak means a win is "due."
Defense: Use structured frameworks to evaluate financial decisions. KeepRule Principles provides evidence-based decision tools for financial choices.

In Social Media

Social media amplifies availability bias (trending = important), appeal to popularity (viral = true), and strawman arguments (context-free quotes). The speed of social media leaves no room for careful reasoning.
Defense: Slow down. Before sharing or reacting, ask: Is this the full story? Is this representative? What is the source? Apply the full reasoning toolkit from KeepRule.
